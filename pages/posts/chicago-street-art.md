# A Love Letter to Chicago Street Art

## Introduction

I've always been a fan of street art. I often don't understand how street artists are often criminalized for their work.  Especially in Chicago, I feel as though the street art that surrounds a neighborhood can tell you so much about the community that lives there, the history of the area, and the people that have lived there. 

Street art is an expression of the people, it's a way to communicate and share stories, it's a way to make a statement, and it's a way to make a neighborhood feel more alive and have personality. 

So, I wanted to share some of my favorite street art pieces in Chicago that I found over the past few years and what they mean to me. I will be updating this post periodically as I find more pieces that I love.

## Milwaukee and Maplewood

![Milwaukee and Maplewood](/images/StreetArt/wicker_train.jpeg "yarn.lock")

This is a piece located in Logan Square though relatively close on the borders of Logan Square, Bucktown, and Wicker Park. 

We can see a 3D depiction of a CTA train. The artwork is designed to look like two subway cars that are connected to each other with the labels "LOGAN" and "SQUARE" on each side (which is where this artwork is located). We can see a sign on top of the train that reads "DO NOT ENTER". In terms of aesthetics, the colors are mostly gray and black. We can also some black outlines of people inside of the train

I love this piece because for one, if you know me personally, you know that I am big fan of the CTA. But I think this piece is a callout to the gentrification that has been happening in Logan Square. For context, Logan Square was once a predominantly Hispanic neighborhood but has been gentrified over the past few years with the influx of young professionals and the construction of luxury apartments and businesses that cater towards more affluent people. In general, Logan Square is essentially an extension of the very hipstery, affluent, and white neighborhoods of Wicker Park and Bucktown (which also used to be predominantly Hispanic neighborhoods).

The **"DO NOT ENTER"** is a subtle callout to the people that have caused displacement and disruption to once thriving community of Logan Square. Although not visible, I see the people inside of the train as the people that have been displaced and the people that have been affected by the gentrification.


## Milwaukee and California

![Milwaukee and California](/images/StreetArt/logan_chase.jpeg "yarn.lock")

This is another piece located more west of the first one that I showed. This piece showcases a colorful mural of a group of figures that appear to present as a Latine family. We can see a woman in a red blouse and two men in suits, with one of them holding a diploma so we can guess that the center figure is a graduate of some sort. 

We also see other colorful artwork that is surrounding the central figures, including popular Chicago landmarks, and Latinx cultural symbols. We can see that this mural is a celebration of Latine accomplishments and culture.

The irony of this though, is that this piece is located on the wall of a Chase bank in a neighborhood that has been heavily affected by gentrification. Perhaps we can guess that this is an institutional attempt at presenting itself as a community-centric bank by acknowledging the community that it is in but, I think it's a slap in the face for the community and folks that have been displaced by institutions such as Chase bank and the gentrification that it has caused. In this case, it's ironic how institutions attempt to identity themselves as allies to the community that they have displaced and disrupted.
